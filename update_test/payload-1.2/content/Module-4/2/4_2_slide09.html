Figure 3: Mode of action of an antigen-detecting malaria rapid diagnostic test (lateral flow assay).

(a—c) Antigen capture and fluid movement along the nitrocellulose membrane. (a) The lateral flow assay consists of a nitrocellulose membrane with bound antibody (Ab) lines (test line and control line) and a pad containing labelled antibodies. The labelled antibodies and the test line antibodies bind to the target antigen.

(b) A small sample of blood, usually 5 to 20 μL from a finger-prick, is placed in a well of the cassette. The blood cells are lysed (opened) by chemicals on the strip to release the antigen (Ag) from within red blood cells and parasites. Antigen present binds to antibodies (Ab) which are labelled with a nanoparticle, usually gold. The complex is flushed up the strip by addition of buffer.

(c) The labelled antibodies accumulate on another line of antibody (test line). If sufficient complex accumulates, the nanoparticles become visible to the eye and the test is considered ‘positive’. A further antibody line (‘control line’) captures a separate marker (usually another antibody) and indicates that flow up the strip has successfully occurred.

Adapted from: Bell, D., C. Wongsrichanalai, et al. (2006). ‘Ensuring quality and access for malaria diagnosis: how can it be achieved?’ Nat Rev Microbiol 4(9): 682-95.
