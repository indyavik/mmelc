Babesia in Blood Films (1 of 3)

Babesia spp. are blood-dwelling protozoan parasites that are morphologically similar to malaria parasites, especially young trophozoites of P. falciparum. The parasites are transmitted by ticks and invade erythrocytes. Only trophozoites and dividing trophozoites are seen in stained blood films.

Symptoms of babesiosis are similar to those of malaria. In stained blood films, the parasites show conspicuous vacuoles, very delicate cytoplasm and red chromatin. The two most common species are B. microti and B. divergens.

Most cases have been reported from North America. Some features that help in differentiating Babesia from malaria parasites are: the presence of rings outside (as well as inside) red blood cells, the absence of other parasite stages, a cross-like formation of dividing trophozoites in red blood cells, and the patient's history that includes travel to North America, excludes travel to malaria-endemic regions or otherwise excludes exposure to malaria.
