Microfilariae in Blood Films (1 of 2)

Microfilariae are the larval forms of several filarial worms that infect humans. The most common species are Wuchereria bancrofti (worldwide), Brugia malayi (Asia), Mansonella spp. (widespread) and Loa loa and Onchocerca volvulus (mainly in Africa). The parasites are transmitted by mosquitoes or biting flies.

Adult worms live in host tissues, lymphatic vessels or skin. Microfilariae are larger than normal components of blood, and are better visualised at lower magnification (x100 or x400 total magnification). For example, the microfilariae of W. bancrofti may be 280 µm long and 25 µm wide. A rapid review of thin blood films at low power microscopy will ensure that these large parasites are not missed.
